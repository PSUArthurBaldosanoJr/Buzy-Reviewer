Discrete Mathematics

Discrete Mathematics is a part of mathematics devoted to the study of discrete objects. Discrete means consisting of distinct or unconnected elements.

It is used whenever objects are being counted, when relationships between finite (countable) sets are studied, and when processes involving a finite number of steps are analyzed.

Importance of Discrete Mathematics

The key reason for the growth in the importance of Discrete Mathematics is that information is stored and manipulated by computing machines in a discrete fashion.

Problems Solved Using Discrete Mathematics

  • How many ways are there to choose a valid password in a computer system?
  • What is the probability of winning a lottery?
  • Is there a link between computers in a network?
  • What is the shortest path between two cities using a transportation system?
  • How can a list of integers be sorted so that they are arranged in increasing order?
  • How many steps are required to do such sorting?
  • How can a circuit that adds two integers be designed?
  • How many valid internet addresses are there?

Why Study Discrete Mathematics?

  • Develops mathematical maturity.
  • Enhances the ability to understand and create mathematical arguments.
  • It is the gateway to more advanced courses in all parts of mathematical sciences.
  • Provides mathematical foundations for many IT and computer science courses, including data structures, algorithms, database theory, automata theory, formal languages, compiler theory, computer security, and operating systems.

Proposition

A proposition is a statement that is either TRUE or FALSE, but not both.

Examples of Propositions:

  • Puerto Princesa City is the capital of the Philippines. (False)
  • 123 - 123 = 0 (True)
  • Washington DC is the capital of the USA. (True)
  • 25 * 25 = 25 (False)

Examples of Statements That Are Not Propositions:

  • What time is it?
  • X + 12345 = 12345
  • X + Y = Z
  • She is beautiful.

Negation

  • Letters are used to denote propositions, just as letters are used to denote variables.
  • The conventional letters used for this purpose are p, q, r, s, ...
  • New propositions, called compound propositions, are formed from existing propositions using logical operators.

Method of producing new propositions

Methods were discussed by the English mathematician George Boole in 1854 in his book the Laws of Thought. Many mathematical statements are constructed by combining one or more propositions.


Let p be a proposition. The statement “It is not the case that p” is another proposition, called the negation of p.

The negation of p is denoted as ¬p and is read as "not p".


Example:

  • Original proposition: Today is Friday.
  • Negation: Today is not Friday.

Important Remarks

  • Sentences involving variable times are not propositions unless a fixed time is assumed.
  • The same holds for variable places unless a fixed place is assumed.
  • Pronouns must refer to a particular person to be considered propositions.

More Examples

  • Original: There are no corrupt people in the Philippines.
    Negation: Some people in the Philippines are corrupt.
  • Original: Dr. Alcid is always late in classes.
    Negation: Dr. Alcid has never been late in classes.
  • Original: No cat can sing.
    Negation: All cats can sing.
  • Original: Mayon Volcano has a perfect cone.
    Negation: Mayon Volcano does not have a perfect cone.

Exclusive OR

The Exclusive OR (XOR) of two propositions, denoted as p ⊕ q, is a logical operation that returns TRUE when exactly one of the two propositions is true and FALSE otherwise.

In simple terms, XOR means "either p or q is true, but not both."

Key Characteristics of XOR

  • Returns TRUE if one of the statements is true and the other is false.
  • Returns FALSE if both statements are either true or false.
  • Unlike the inclusive OR (p ∨ q), XOR ensures that both conditions cannot be true simultaneously.

Example of Exclusive OR

Consider a menu at a restaurant that states: "Soup or salad comes for free."

The restaurant intends to say that customers can have either soup or salad, but not both.

This is an example of an Exclusive OR, as opposed to an Inclusive OR, where getting both items would be allowed.

Comparison: XOR vs. OR

The difference between Exclusive OR (XOR) and Inclusive OR lies in the case when both propositions are true:

  • Inclusive OR (p ∨ q): TRUE when either or both statements are true.
  • Exclusive OR (p ⊕ q): TRUE only when one statement is true, but not both.

Application of XOR

XOR is widely used in **computer science** and **digital logic**:

  • In cryptography, XOR is a fundamental operation in encryption algorithms.
  • In computer arithmetic, XOR is used in half-adders and full-adders for binary addition.
  • In error detection, XOR is used in parity checks to detect data corruption.


Implication

In logic, an implication is a proposition that establishes a conditional relationship between two statements. It is denoted as p → q, meaning "if p, then q."

Here, p is called the hypothesis (or antecedent), and q is called the conclusion (or consequence).

Ways to Express an Implication

  • If p, then q
  • p implies q
  • p only if q
  • p is sufficient for q
  • q if p
  • q whenever p
  • q is necessary for p

Understanding Implication in Real Life

A useful way to understand implications is by thinking of them as contracts or obligations. If the condition is not met, there is no obligation to fulfill the consequence.

Example 1

Statement: "If you make more than Php 25,000.00, then you must file an income tax return."

  • This does not say anything about those earning less than Php 25,000.00.
  • It is violated only if someone earns more than Php 25,000.00 and does not file a tax return.

Example 2

Statement: "If a baseball player hits more than 60 home runs, then they receive a bonus of 1 million pesos."

  • If the player hits more than 60 home runs but does not receive the bonus, the implication is violated.
  • If they hit fewer than 60 home runs, the rule does not apply, so the statement remains true.

Implication in Programming

In programming, the IF-THEN structure follows a different logic. If the condition p is true, the statement s is executed. If p is false, nothing happens.

Example:

            x = 0
            If (2 + 2 = 4) then x := x + 1

Since 2 + 2 = 4 is true, the statement x := x + 1 is executed, making x = 1.

Converse and Contrapositive

Additional logical statements can be derived from an implication p → q.

Converse

The converse of p → q is q → p. This means "if q, then p."

Contrapositive

The contrapositive of p → q is ¬q → ¬p. This means "if not q, then not p."

Example:

Original Statement: "If today is Thursday, then I have a test today."

  • Converse: "If I have a test today, then today is Thursday."
  • Contrapositive: "If I do not have a test today, then today is not Thursday."

Logical Operators and Parentheses

Logical expressions often contain multiple operators. Parentheses are used to specify the order of operations.

Examples:

  • (p ∨ q) ∧ (¬r) → This is the conjunction of p ∨ q and ¬r.
  • ¬p ∧ q → This is the conjunction of ¬p and q, written as (¬p) ∧ q.
  • ¬(p ∧ q) → This is NOT the same as (¬p) ∧ q.
